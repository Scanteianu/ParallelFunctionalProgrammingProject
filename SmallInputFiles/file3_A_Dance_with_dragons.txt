<p><i><b>A Dance with Dragons</b></i> is the fifth novel, of seven planned, in the <a href="/wiki/Epic_fantasy" class="mw-redirect" title="Epic fantasy">epic fantasy</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire" title="A Song of Ice and Fire">A Song of Ice and Fire</a></i> by American author <a href="/wiki/George_R._R._Martin" title="George R. R. Martin">George R. R. Martin</a>.  In some areas, the paperback edition was published in two parts, titled <i>Dreams and Dust</i> and <a href="/wiki/After_the_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="After the Festival"><i>After the Feast</i>.</a> It was the only novel in the series to be published during the eight-season run of the <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> adaptation of the series, <i><a href="/wiki/Game_of_Thrones" title="Game of Thrones">Game of Thrones</a></i>, and runs to 1,040 pages with a word count of almost 415,000.
</p><p>The US hardcover was officially published on July 12, 2011, and a few weeks later went to No. 1 on both <i><a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">Publishers Weekly</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> bestsellers lists.  The novel has been adapted for television as the <a href="/wiki/Game_of_Thrones_(season_5)" title="Game of Thrones (season 5)">fifth season</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Game_of_Thrones" title="Game of Thrones">Game of Thrones</a></i>, although elements of the book have also appeared in the series' third, fourth and sixth seasons.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Wall_and_beyond">The Wall and beyond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Wall and beyond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stannis_Baratheon" title="Stannis Baratheon">Stannis Baratheon</a>, a claimant to the Iron Throne of <a href="/wiki/Westeros" class="mw-redirect" title="Westeros">Westeros</a>, occupies <a href="/wiki/The_Wall_(A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Wall (A Song of Ice and Fire)">the Wall</a> at the realm's northern border, having helped to repel an invasion of <a href="/wiki/Wildling_(character)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildling (character)">wildlings</a> from the northern wilderness. Stannis executes <a href="/wiki/Mance_Rayder" class="mw-redirect" title="Mance Rayder">Mance Rayder</a>, the leader of the wildlings, for refusing to submit to him, and marches his army south to seek support in his bid for the throne.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jon_Snow_(character)" title="Jon Snow (character)">Jon Snow</a>, the newly elected Lord Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Night%27s_Watch" class="mw-redirect" title="Night&#39;s Watch">Night's Watch</a>, the order that defends the Wall, prepares the defense against the <a href="/wiki/Others_(A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Others (A Song of Ice and Fire)">Others</a>, hostile inhuman creatures from the far north. Jon negotiates with wildling leader <a href="/wiki/Tormund_Giantsbane" title="Tormund Giantsbane">Tormund Giantsbane</a> to let the wildlings past the Wall in exchange for their assistance in defending it. This results in a fragile peace but creates unrest among the Night's Watch, who have considered the wildlings their enemies for centuries.
</p><p>Stannis's advisor, the sorceress <a href="/wiki/Melisandre" title="Melisandre">Melisandre</a>, warns Jon that his half-sister, <a href="/wiki/Arya_Stark" title="Arya Stark">Arya Stark</a>, is in trouble. Mance is revealed to be alive, thanks to Melisandre's magical trickery. He is sent to the Starks' ancestral castle <a href="/wiki/Winterfell" class="mw-redirect" title="Winterfell">Winterfell</a>, now occupied by the enemy Boltons, to rescue Arya. However, the girl in Melisandre's visions turns out to be Alys Karstark, a young noblewoman fleeing to the Wall to escape her treacherous uncle. To protect Alys and aid the wildlings' integration into Westerosi society, Jon arranges for Alys to marry Sigorn of Thenn, a wildling leader.
</p><p>Jon receives a taunting letter from <a href="/wiki/Ramsay_Bolton" title="Ramsay Bolton">Ramsay Bolton</a>, who claims to have crushed Stannis's army at Winterfell. Threatening attack, Ramsay demands that Jon send him Stannis's wife, daughter, and other hostages, in addition to Arya and Jon's foster-brother <a href="/wiki/Theon_Greyjoy" title="Theon Greyjoy">Theon Greyjoy</a>, the latter two whom Jon hasn't seen in years. Jon decides to march on Ramsay himself and asks for volunteers to accompany him. His officers view Jon's intent to march on Ramsay as a betrayal of the Watch's neutrality and he is stabbed in a mutiny.
</p><p>Meanwhile, Jon's crippled half-brother <a href="/wiki/Bran_Stark" title="Bran Stark">Bran Stark</a>, traveling north of the Wall, is led to the last surviving <a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Children of the Forest">Children of the Forest</a>, the non-human natives of Westeros. They introduce Bran and his companions to the "last greenseer", an ancient man intertwined with the roots of a weirwood tree. He helps Bran learn <a href="/wiki/Clairvoyance" title="Clairvoyance">clairvoyant</a> "greensight", enabling Bran to witness the past and present through the eyes of the weirwood trees that grow throughout the North.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Across_the_Narrow_Sea">Across the Narrow Sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Across the Narrow Sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Free_Cities">In the Free Cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In the Free Cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Having killed his father <a href="/wiki/Tywin_Lannister" title="Tywin Lannister">Tywin</a>, the Hand of the King, the dwarf <a href="/wiki/Tyrion_Lannister" title="Tyrion Lannister">Tyrion Lannister</a> is smuggled out of Westeros to the city of <a href="/wiki/Pentos_(A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pentos (A Song of Ice and Fire)">Pentos</a> by the spymaster <a href="/wiki/Varys" title="Varys">Varys</a>, where he is sheltered by the merchant <a href="/wiki/Illyrio_Mopatis" class="mw-redirect" title="Illyrio Mopatis">Illyrio Mopatis</a>. Tyrion is sent south with a party ostensibly to aid the exiled princess <a href="/wiki/Daenerys_Targaryen" title="Daenerys Targaryen">Daenerys Targaryen</a>, who controls the only living <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">dragons</a>, in claiming the Iron Throne. Tyrion learns that Varys and Illyrio have hidden the presumed-dead <a href="/wiki/Aegon_VI_Targaryen" class="mw-redirect" title="Aegon VI Targaryen">Aegon Targaryen</a>, son of Daenerys's late brother Prince <a href="/wiki/Rhaegar_Targaryen" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhaegar Targaryen">Rhaegar</a>, and intend to install him as king of Westeros with Daenerys's aid. Tyrion persuades Aegon to invade Westeros immediately, without waiting for Daenerys. Soon afterward, Tyrion is kidnapped by Daenerys's disgraced advisor <a href="/wiki/Jorah_Mormont" title="Jorah Mormont">Jorah Mormont</a>, who intends to deliver him to Daenerys in order to regain her favor.
</p><p>In the Free City of Braavos, Arya is an acolyte of the guild of assassins known as the Faceless Men. When they temporarily afflict her with blindness, Arya develops her sense of hearing, and realizes that she can "see" through cats the same way she could with her pet direwolf, Nymeria. After her sight is restored, Arya is tasked with assassinating a corrupt merchant while magically disguised with another girl's face. After doing so, Arya is declared an apprentice of the Faceless Men, to be sent to another assassin to continue her training.
</p>
<h4><span id="Slaver.27s_Bay"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Slaver's_Bay">Slaver's Bay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Slaver&#039;s Bay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Daenerys has conquered the city of <a href="/wiki/Meereen" class="mw-redirect" title="Meereen">Meereen</a> and banned slavery, but struggles to maintain peace among various factions within the city, including the Sons of the Harpy, a violent Meereenese resistance group, and with the neighboring city of <a href="/wiki/Yunkai" class="mw-redirect" title="Yunkai">Yunkai</a>.
</p><p>Daenerys' dragons have become increasingly dangerous, and she reluctantly confines them in a dungeon for the safety of her people. Drogon, the largest, evades capture and flies off. Despite her sexual relationship with the mercenary <a href="/wiki/Daario_Naharis" title="Daario Naharis">Daario Naharis</a>, Daenerys marries the Meereenese nobleman <a href="/wiki/Hizdahr_zo_Loraq" class="mw-redirect" title="Hizdahr zo Loraq">Hizdahr zo Loraq</a> to secure an alliance to appease the Sons of the Harpy.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Quentyn_Martell" class="mw-redirect" title="Quentyn Martell">Quentyn Martell</a>, the son of the Prince of Dorne in southern Westeros, arrives in Meereen in hopes of renewing the alliance between Daenerys's family and his, but he is unable to maintain her attention. Jorah and Tyrion are shipwrecked, kidnapped, and sold to a Yunkish slave trader. Tyrion escapes from the Yunkish army besieging Meereen, joins the Second Sons mercenary group, and secures their support for Daenerys. Meanwhile, another Westerosi, <a href="/wiki/Victarion_Greyjoy" class="mw-redirect" title="Victarion Greyjoy">Victarion Greyjoy</a>, sails for Meereen, intending to marry Daenerys and use her dragons to overthrow his brother, the king of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Islands">Ironborn</a>.
</p><p>At Hizdahr's insistence, Daenerys reopens the city's "fighting pits" for <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiatorial</a> combat, but the noise and carnage attract Drogon. In the resulting chaos, Daenerys climbs onto Drogon to calm him but he flies away with her. Hizdahr is implicated in an attempt to poison Daenerys. Her advisor, <a href="/wiki/Barristan_Selmy" class="mw-redirect" title="Barristan Selmy">Barristan Selmy</a>, removes Hizdahr from power, and the Sons of the Harpy resume their killing. Barristan prepares for battle with the armies outside Meereen. Quentyn attempts to prove his worth by riding one of the remaining dragons but is killed and both dragons are released.
</p><p>Drogon flies Daenerys to the <a href="/wiki/Dothraki_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Dothraki Sea">Dothraki Sea</a>, the grassland controlled by the nomadic Dothraki people. After several days, Daenerys encounters a Dothraki horde led by Khal Jhaqo.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Seven_Kingdoms">In the Seven Kingdoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In the Seven Kingdoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_North">The North</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The North">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/The_North_(A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire)" class="mw-redirect" title="The North (A Song of Ice and Fire)">the North</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roose_Bolton" title="Roose Bolton">Roose Bolton</a> has overthrown House Stark and assumed authority as the Warden of the North after allying with <a href="/wiki/House_Frey" class="mw-redirect" title="House Frey">House Frey</a>.  Much of the region is occupied by Ironborn invaders.  Under Jon Snow's advice, Stannis wins the support of northern mountain clans by pledging to recapture Winterfell and fighting off the Ironborn.  With their support, Stannis captures <a href="/wiki/Asha_Greyjoy" class="mw-redirect" title="Asha Greyjoy">Asha Greyjoy</a>, Victarion's niece, and marches his forces toward Winterfell to attack the Boltons, but his advance is halted by heavy <a href="/wiki/Snowstorm" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowstorm">snowstorms</a>.
</p><p>Stannis's advisor <a href="/wiki/Davos_Seaworth" title="Davos Seaworth">Davos Seaworth</a> is sent to win the support of the wealthy lord Wyman Manderly, who pretends to execute Davos to curry favor with the <a href="/wiki/Lannister" class="mw-redirect" title="Lannister">Lannister</a> regime that controls the Iron Throne. In a secret meeting, Manderly tells Davos that he and other Northern vassals intend to feign submission to the Boltons and Lannisters while plotting revenge for <a href="/wiki/Robb_Stark" title="Robb Stark">Robb Stark</a>'s death and the restoration of Stark rule.  Revealing to Davos that young <a href="/wiki/Rickon_Stark" title="Rickon Stark">Rickon Stark</a> is in hiding on the remote island of Skagos, Manderly pledges to support Stannis if Davos can retrieve Rickon and unite the Starks' supporters around him.
</p><p>Theon Greyjoy is a prisoner of the Boltons, and is mutilated and tortured by Roose's sadistic son Ramsay, who renames Theon "Reek".  To cement his rule over the North, Roose has Ramsay supposedly married to Arya Stark, but the girl is actually <a href="/wiki/Jeyne_Poole" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeyne Poole">Jeyne Poole</a>, a friend of Arya's sister <a href="/wiki/Sansa_Stark" title="Sansa Stark">Sansa</a>, who is forced to impersonate Arya and is physically and sexually tormented by Ramsay.  A disguised Mance Rayder arrives at Winterfell and enlists Theon to help him free the false Arya.  Theon and Jeyne escape, leaping from the castle wall into the snow to be captured by Stannis's forces.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_South">The South</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The South">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jaime_Lannister" title="Jaime Lannister">Jaime Lannister</a>, the uncle (and, secretly, father) of the young king <a href="/wiki/Tommen_Baratheon" title="Tommen Baratheon">Tommen Baratheon</a>, negotiates the surrender of the last of the late Robb Stark's allies, nominally putting an end to the Starkâ€“Lannister war in the <a href="/wiki/Riverlands_(A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Riverlands (A Song of Ice and Fire)">Riverlands</a>. <a href="/wiki/Brienne_of_Tarth" title="Brienne of Tarth">Brienne of Tarth</a>, whom Jaime had sent to search for Sansa Stark, finds Jaime and tells him Sansa is in danger; he follows after her.
</p><p>After Tyrion convinces Aegon to attack Westeros, Aegon and his forces capture several castles in the Stormlands with little resistance. Meanwhile, Aegon's foster father <a href="/wiki/Jon_Connington" class="mw-redirect" title="Jon Connington">Jon Connington</a> is secretly succumbing to greyscale, a deadly and infectious disease.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Doran_Martell" class="mw-redirect" title="Doran Martell">Doran Martell</a>, the prince of Dorne, sends three of his nieces north to the capital city of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Landing_(A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire)" class="mw-redirect" title="King&#39;s Landing (A Song of Ice and Fire)">King's Landing</a> to infiltrate the Faith and the government and work in Dorne's interests.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cersei_Lannister" title="Cersei Lannister">Cersei Lannister</a>, Tommen's mother and queen regent, has been arrested by the Faith on charges of fornication and conspiracy. To gain release from her imprisonment, she confesses to several of the lesser charges against her, but does not confess to having murdered her husband King <a href="/wiki/Robert_Baratheon" title="Robert Baratheon">Robert Baratheon</a>, nor that her children are the product of incest. As a condition of her release, Cersei is forced to humiliate herself by <a href="/wiki/Perp_walk#Prehistory" title="Perp walk">walking naked across the city</a>. Meanwhile, Cersei's ally, the ex-maester <a href="/wiki/Qyburn" class="mw-redirect" title="Qyburn">Qyburn</a>, has created "Ser Robert Strong", an eight-foot-tall figure encased in armor, to be an unbeatable champion in Cersei's upcoming <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_combat" title="Trial by combat">trial by combat</a>.
</p><p>Having taken control of the government, Cersei's uncle <a href="/wiki/Kevan_Lannister" class="mw-redirect" title="Kevan Lannister">Kevan Lannister</a> and the Grand Maester <a href="/wiki/Pycelle" class="mw-redirect" title="Pycelle">Pycelle</a> attempt to undo the damage caused by Cersei's misrule. However, Varys reappears and murders both Kevan and Pycelle, revealing that he has been plotting for years for the Lannisters to destroy themselves so that Aegon Targaryen can take the throne, having been raised to be an ideal ruler. The book ends with Varys sending his child spies to finish Kevan off.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>A Dance with Dragons</i> was originally intended to be the title of the second novel in the sequence, when Martin still envisioned the series as a <a href="/wiki/Trilogy" title="Trilogy">trilogy</a>. Some early US editions of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Game_of_Thrones" title="A Game of Thrones">A Game of Thrones</a></i> (1996) list <i>A Dance of Dragons</i> as the forthcoming second volume in the series. The 1998 anthology <i><a href="/wiki/Legends_(book)" title="Legends (book)">Legends</a></i>, which features the novella <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hedge_Knight" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hedge Knight">The Hedge Knight</a></i> from the same universe, listed <i>A Dance of Dragons</i> as the third installment of a four-book series.
</p><p>In May 2005 Martin announced that the "sheer size" of his still-unfinished manuscript for <i>A Feast for Crows</i> had led him and his publishers to split the narrative into two books.<sup id="cite_ref-Done_2005_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Done_2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Rather than divide the text in half chronologically, Martin opted to instead split the material by character and location, resulting in "two novels taking place simultaneously" with different casts of characters.<sup id="cite_ref-Done_2005_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Done_2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Published in 2005, <i>A Feast for Crows</i> is narrated primarily by characters in the South of the Seven Kingdoms and in the new locations of the Iron Islands and Dorne. <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> features characters in the North and <a href="/wiki/Across_the_narrow_sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Across the narrow sea">across the narrow sea</a>, although Jaime Lannister, Cersei Lannister, Arya Stark, Areo Hotah and Victarion and Asha Greyjoy appear in both volumes.
</p><p>Approximately one-third of the published <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> consists of material that had been written for the pre-split <i>A Feast for Crows</i>, although much of this has been rewritten by Martin.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, Martin confirmed that, contrary to earlier statements, <a href="/wiki/Sansa_Stark" title="Sansa Stark">Sansa Stark</a> would not appear in <i>A Dance with Dragons</i>; Sansa chapters initially slated for the novel have instead been pushed back to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Winds_of_Winter" title="The Winds of Winter">The Winds of Winter</a></i>, which is planned to be the sixth book in the series.<sup id="cite_ref-LJ_2009-02-09_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LJ_2009-02-09-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In early 2010, Martin noted that his intent for <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> was for the first 800 manuscript pages to cover the alternate characters in the same time span as <i>A Feast for Crows</i>, and that "Everything that follows is post-<i>Feast</i>, so that's where some of the cast from the last book start popping up again."<sup id="cite_ref-LJ_2010-02-15_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LJ_2010-02-15-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Stating that "I wanted to resolve at least a few of the cliffhangers from <i>Feast</i>," Martin also mentioned the possibility that some of his finished chapters might get pushed to the next novel, <i>The Winds of Winter,</i> depending on the length of the finished manuscript for <i>A Dance with Dragons</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-LJ_2010-02-15_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LJ_2010-02-15-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_to_publication">Road to publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Road to publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite original, optimistic predictions of possible completion in late 2006, Martin completed the novel in April 2011, nearly five years later. During this period, Martin's blog featured sporadic updates on his progress, and in January 2008 he posted an update affirming his vigilant commitment to finishing the novel.<sup id="cite_ref-Update_2008-01_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Update_2008-01-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In early 2008, publisher <a href="/wiki/Spectra_Books" class="mw-redirect" title="Spectra Books">Spectra Books</a> (a division of <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>) announced that <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> would be released on September 30, 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> but Martin stated this would only be possible if he finished writing by the end of June, before his trip to Spain and Portugal;<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> he did not meet his goal.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 19, 2009, Martin posted on his website: "I am trying to finish the book by June. I think I can do that. If I do, <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> will likely be published in September or October."<sup id="cite_ref-LJ_2009-02-09_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LJ_2009-02-09-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On June 22, 2009, the author expressed "guarded optimism" with respect to his progress on the novel, while still not confirming a publication date.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> When asked in a July 2009 interview with <i>FREE! Magazine</i> how the book was going, Martin stated, "It is going pretty well, actually. I am hoping to finish it by September or October; that is my goal."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> On October 6, 2009, Martin said that his working manuscript for <i>A Dance With Dragons</i> had just exceeded 1,100 pages of completed chapters, plus "considerably more in partials, fragments, and roughs."<sup id="cite_ref-LJ_2009-10-06_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LJ_2009-10-06-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He noted that this made the upcoming novel longer than his earlier books <i><a href="/wiki/A_Game_of_Thrones" title="A Game of Thrones">A Game of Thrones</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Feast_for_Crows" title="A Feast for Crows">A Feast for Crows</a></i>, and nearly as long as <i><a href="/wiki/A_Clash_of_Kings" title="A Clash of Kings">A Clash of Kings</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-LJ_2009-10-06_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LJ_2009-10-06-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 2, 2010, Martin remarked that he had reached 1,311 manuscript pages, making <i>Dance</i> the second-longest novel in the series at that point, behind only the 1,521-page manuscript of <i>A Storm of Swords</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-LJ_2010-03-02_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LJ_2010-03-02-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> On July 8, 2010, Martin spoke at a conference and confirmed the current length of the book to be 1,400 manuscript pages. He expressed his disappointment that he was unable to completely finish the novel by the conference, although he would not speculate how soon the book would be completed after his return home on July 11.<sup id="cite_ref-asoiaf.westeros.org_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asoiaf.westeros.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> At the same conference, Martin also confirmed that he has written one Sansa, one Arya, and two Arianne chapters for the planned sixth novel, <i>Winds of Winter</i>, and had transferred two Cersei chapters from that book into <i>A Dance with Dragons</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-asoiaf.westeros.org_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asoiaf.westeros.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> On August 7, 2010, Martin confirmed that he had completed eight POVs, excluding the prologue and epilogue.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Comic_Con" title="New York Comic Con">New York Comic Con</a> on  October 10, 2010, Spectra senior editor Anne Groell announced that Martin had only five chapters remaining to finish, with sections of the chapters already completed. She stated her desire to have the manuscript completed by December.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In a December 2010 interview with Bear Swarm, Martin stated that he almost had <i>A Dance With Dragons</i> completed.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 3, 2011, the publisher announced that the novel, though at that point still not completed, would be officially published on July 12, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Dragon_Release_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dragon_Release-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Good_news_for_old_blighty_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Good_news_for_old_blighty-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Martin claimed this July 2011 publication date was different from the previous publication dates mentioned, in that this was "real", as opposed to earlier "wishful thinking, boundless optimism, cockeyed dreams, [and] honest mistakes".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> On March 12, he revealed that the unfinished manuscript had exceeded <i>A Storm of Swords</i> in length, making it the longest volume in the entire series.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> On March 27, he announced that the manuscript had exceeded 1,600 pages.<sup id="cite_ref-http&#58;//grrm.livejournal.com/204875.html_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http://grrm.livejournal.com/204875.html-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> On April 27, 2011, it was announced that Martin had completed <i>A Dance with Dragons</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> After incorporating requested changes made by his book editor and her staff copy-editors, suggested final draft notes from trusted friends, and his own final "sweat" (a line-by-line reread done to tighten and eliminate any unnecessary "fat" remaining in the manuscript), the final draft had been reduced to 1,510 pages; this made <i>Dragons</i> the second-longest novel, by a narrow margin, to volume three, <i>A Storm of Swords</i>. The novel was now ready to meet its July 12, 2011 release date.
</p><p>On June 29, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Germany">Amazon Germany</a> had mistakenly released 180 copies of the novel early. Martin requested that those who held copies not spoil the book for fans who had to wait. Several <i><a href="/wiki/A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire" title="A Song of Ice and Fire">A Song of Ice and Fire</a></i> websites put an embargo in place on their forums with the same intent.<sup id="cite_ref-AmazonPooch_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmazonPooch-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> Rachael Brown found <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> "infinitely more satisfying than its predecessor, 2005's bleak and plodding <i>A Feast for Crows</i>. The aspects of Martin's work that have endeared him to fans are abundant here â€“ rich world building, narrative twists and turns, and gritty depictions of the human struggle for power. Characters who were sorely missed in <i>Feast</i> â€“ Daenerys Targaryen, Tyrion Lannister, and Jon Snow â€“ make up more than a third of the novel, and Martin is wise enough to give us at least a chapter from (almost) everyone else. Weaknesses that have plagued Martin's previous books are also present: too much repetition, unexceptional prose, and characters who use the same idioms (and have sex in exactly the same manner) no matter their ethnicity, social class, or continent. But while <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> cries out for better editing, it remains entirely engrossing. Martin has hidden so many clues and red herrings throughout his previous volumes that it is a thrill to see certain pieces fall into place."<sup id="cite_ref-atlantic_sex_fantasy_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlantic_sex_fantasy-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Remy Verhoeve of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> said, "<i>A Dance with Dragons</i> is just a book, of course. It is not the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">Second Coming</a> or anything. And I understand that the author must feel a lot of pressure concerning this one (he should). It's late and it has to repair some of the damage done by <i>A Feast for Crows</i>, which frankly felt as if it was written by a ghost writer at times. Finally it is here, and some of the things we've been wondering about for more than a decade are actually revealed (not everything, but at least some things). It has the same structural problems as the previous book; it is sprawling and incoherent at times, but at least the characters are more interesting than in the previous installment. It does feel like I'm reading a bunch of separate stories within the same settingâ€“the chapters are told through the eyes of various charactersâ€“but that doesn't really bother me as I love the setting and like to see it through various points of view. Theories that have been bandied about online for the last ten years mostly come true in this book, so in that respect the novel isn't shocking but neither is it disappointing. Martin also manages to put in a few twists, but ends the book much like he did the previous one with cliffhangers instead of wrapping things up a little better so the next long wait won't hurt so much."<sup id="cite_ref-huffington_lovehate_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffington_lovehate-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>David Orr of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> said, "<i>A Dance With Dragons</i> comes in at roughly 9,574,622,012 pages, and smart money says the final two books in the series will make this one look like '<a href="/wiki/The_Old_Man_and_the_Sea" title="The Old Man and the Sea">The Old Man and the Sea</a>'. Such length isn't necessary, and it hurts Martin's prose and his plot mechanics. Tyrion 'waddles' at least 12 times here, and even if we suppose the unflattering word reflects Tyrion's contempt for his own awkward gait, it seems unlikely he would indulge this contempt when he's, say, fighting for his life. Similarly, when your novel's terrain stretches across hundreds of miles and your world lacks jet propulsion, as an author you face some basic problems of transportation that can result in conveyance via <a href="/wiki/Rube_Goldberg" title="Rube Goldberg">Rube Goldberg</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_rise_fantasy_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_rise_fantasy-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> James Poniewozik found a possible "weakness to the early Meereen sections [by] marking time (and making Dany uncharacteristically indecisive) to allow time for all the pieces to fall into place" and said, "All this makes for a thousand-page book that feels half as long, that moves dextrously, answers key questions and gobsmacks you with convincing feints and change-ups. As in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Feast_for_Crows" title="A Feast for Crows">AFFC</a></i>, there are sections that feel like they could have used an editor. In some chapters you suddenly find yourself in a strange land with a character you have little attachment to, wondering where this thread is going, as if you had stayed too long at a party after the friends you came with have left.<sup id="cite_ref-time_problem_power_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time_problem_power-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> Bill Sheehan said, "Filled with vividly rendered set pieces, unexpected turnings, assorted cliffhangers and moments of appalling cruelty, <i>A Dance With Dragons</i> is epic fantasy as it should be written: passionate, compelling, convincingly detailed and thoroughly imagined. Despite a number of overtly fantastic elements (dragons, seers, shape shifters and sorcerers), the bookâ€”and the series as a wholeâ€”feels grounded in the brutal reality of medieval times and has more in common with the Wars of the Roses than it does with <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>. The result is a complex summer blockbuster with brains and heart, a book with rareâ€”and potentially enormousâ€”appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-washingtonpost_worth_wait_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost_worth_wait-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Megan Wasson of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Science_Monitor" title="The Christian Science Monitor">The Christian Science Monitor</a></i> said, "<i>A Dance with Dragons</i> may well be one of the best books in the five-book series so far. Martin's prose is concise but pithy, begging to be devoured over and over again. All the fans' favorite characters make an appearance, unlike in the last book. But what truly sets this book above some of the others in the series is Martin's ability to keep his readers on their toes and the edges of their seats. No character, no matter how likeable and seemingly important, is ever safe from Martin's pen (remember Ned Stark?), and on the other hand, no one can ever be truly pronounced dead. What you thought was going to happen after reading Books No. 1, 2, and even 3 and 4, now clearly will not happen, and that's where Martin's strength lies."<sup id="cite_ref-csmonitor_adwdreview_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csmonitor_adwdreview-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Dance_with_Dragons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2012, <i>A Dance with Dragons</i> was nominated for the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Novel" title="Hugo Award for Best Novel">Hugo Award for Best Novel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>  In May 2012, it was nominated for the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Locus_Award_for_Best_Fantasy_Novel" title="Locus Award for Best Fantasy Novel">Locus Award for Best Fantasy Novel</a> and won it in June 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In August 2012, the novel was nominated for a World Fantasy Award for Best Novel.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
